The Problem
Why a flat percentage doesn't work
A flat 1-2% commission sounds simple, but it breaks in prediction markets.
❌

Traps users at low prices

If your position drops to 2 cents and the fee is 1%, the fee can be worth more than the position itself. Users can't afford to exit.

❌

Wipes out small profits

Buying at 95 cents to win 5 cents profit? A 1% fee on $0.95 wipes out most of that 5-cent upside. High-confidence bets become pointless.

❌

No incentive to add liquidity

Both sides of a trade pay the same fee. There's no reward for the user who posts an order and waits, making the market deeper for everyone.

Our Solution
Three principles behind our commission
The same model used by Kalshi (the largest US-regulated prediction market, $263M revenue in 2025), adapted for our platform.
1

Fee scales with uncertainty

At 50/50 odds (maximum uncertainty), the fee is at its highest. As a contract moves toward 0 or 100, the fee shrinks toward zero. This means: cheap contracts have tiny fees, expensive/sure-thing contracts have tiny fees, and mid-range contracts have the highest fees.

2

Active traders pay more, patient traders pay less

If you hit someone else's resting order (you want to trade now), you pay a standard fee. If you post an order and wait for someone to come to you, you pay 1/3 the fee. This rewards users who make the market deeper.

3

Fee is charged only when you exit

Entering a trade is free. You only pay commission when you sell your position or when the event resolves. If you lose and get $0, your fee is $0 too — we never charge more than your payout.

4

Fee can never exceed your payout

Mathematically guaranteed. As your position drops in value, the fee drops even faster. The maximum fee on any trade is 5.7% of what you invested — and that only happens on extreme longshots. At typical prices, it's 1-3%.

Why not charge 10% on winnings?

It sounds simple — "you only pay if you win." But the math is brutal for cheap contracts:

Buy at 50 cents, win

Profit = 50 cents. Fee = 5 cents.

Effective rate: 10%

Buy at 10 cents, win

Profit = 90 cents. Fee = 9 cents.

Effective rate: 90% of investment

Buy at 5 cents, win

Profit = 95 cents. Fee = 9.5 cents.

Effective rate: 190% of investment!

With our model, a 5-cent contract has a commission of just $0.29 (5.7%). Everyone can trade at any price.

Fee Reference Price
Two approaches — which one should we use?
The fee formula is the same. The only question: when a user sells before settlement, which price does the bell curve use?
VERSION 1

Sell Price Model

Bell curve uses the current market price at the time of exit.

Sell exit: fee based on sell price
Settlement: fee based on entry price
  • Fee always proportional to what the user receives right now
  • User is never trapped — fee shrinks as position value shrinks
  • Opens the door to churn-to-reset exploit by bots
  • Systematically lower revenue when prices move toward extremes
VERSION 2

Entry Price Model

Bell curve always uses the price the user originally paid.

Sell exit: fee based on entry price, capped at proceeds
Settlement: fee based on entry price, capped at payout
  • Fee is locked in at entry — user knows their fee from day one
  • Churn exploit is dead — selling and re-buying costs more
  • Higher platform revenue in most scenarios
  • Cap at proceeds prevents trapping — see solution below

SOLUTION How V2 prevents trapping users

The one risk with entry price: if the price crashes far below entry, the fee (based on entry) could exceed the sell proceeds. The fix is simple — cap the fee at the proceeds.

Why the cap barely affects revenue
  • People come to profit, not lose. A position at $0.01 means the user lost 98% of their investment. The difference between a $0.015 fee and a $0.01 fee is half a cent — irrelevant against a $0.49 loss.
  • Cap only triggers at extreme crashes. For the cap to activate, the sell price must be far below entry. This is a rare edge case, not the common path.
  • Revenue impact: effectively zero. The lost revenue per contract is $0.005 on a position worth $0.01. Even at scale, this rounds to nothing.
  • It protects the user experience. A user who just lost 98% should be able to exit cleanly for whatever their position is worth — not pay a fee they can't afford.

FAQ
Common questions
How much commission will I pay?

It depends on the contract price. At 50/50 odds, the initiating trader pays about 3% and the waiting trader pays about 1%. At extreme prices (near 0 or near $1), both pay close to 0%. The commission is always shown before you confirm the trade.

When is the commission charged?

Only when you exit. Entering a trade is always free. You pay commission when you sell your position or when the event resolves. If you lose and your payout is $0, you pay $0 in commission.

Why do two people in the same trade pay different amounts?

Two reasons. First, the person who initiates ("I want to trade now") pays more than the person whose order was waiting. Second, the Yes buyer and No buyer pay the same dollar amount but different percentages, because one side costs more to enter than the other.

Can the commission ever be more than my payout?

No. This is mathematically impossible with our model. As your position drops in value, the commission drops even faster. The maximum commission on any single trade is about 5.7% of what you invested (on extreme longshots). At typical prices, it's 1-3%.

How do I pay less commission?

Post a limit order instead of buying at the current price. If your order sits on the book and someone else matches it, you're the "patient" trader and you pay 1/3 the commission. This also helps the market by adding depth to the order book.

Is this the same model as Kalshi?

Same core approach (commission scales with market uncertainty, different rates for initiating vs. waiting traders). Our version charges at exit only (Kalshi charges at trade time) and our commission rates are tuned for a ~2% platform target.

Why not just charge a flat percentage?

A flat percentage breaks at extreme prices. If a contract drops to 2 cents, a 1% fee on the $1 contract value can be worth more than the position itself — the user literally can't afford to exit. Our model avoids this by automatically shrinking the commission as prices approach 0 or $1.
